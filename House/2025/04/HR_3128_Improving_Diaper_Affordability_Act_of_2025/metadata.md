Short title\nThis Act may be cited as the Improving Diaper Affordability Act of 2025 .\n#### 2. Findings\nCongress finds the following:\n**(1)**\nDiapers are an essential need for the health and hygiene of babies and toddlers.\n**(2)**\nFamilies without adequate access to diapers face a higher risk of poor health outcomes for their children, including diaper rash, severe dermatitis, infections, and hospitalization, while also posing a significant risk to maternal mental health.\n**(3)**\nFamilies with diaper need lack a sufficient supply of diapers to keep a baby or toddler clean, dry, and healthy.\n**(4)**\n46 percent of families with children age 4 or below struggle to provide the diapers their children need; this is an increase from 1 in 3 families expressing diaper need before the COVID\u201319 pandemic.\n**(5)**\nLow-income families make up 2 out of 3 families with diaper need; a disproportionate amount of these families are Black or Latino.\n**(6)**\nAnnually, families spend between $945 and $1,500 on diapers for a child under age 4; a low-income family of 4 spends 2.6 times more of their income on diaper costs compared to a family of 4 making median income.\n**(7)**\nDiaper need affects not only the health and well-being of the child but it can affect the economic security of the entire family, forcing families with diaper need to cut back on food purchases and personal savings.\n**(8)**\n25 percent of parents and caregivers with diaper need reported having to miss work or school because they did not have enough diapers to drop their child off at childcare, and reported missing, on average, 5.1 workdays in the past 30 days; this represents a loss of $296 per month for a parent earning the Federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour.\n**(9)**\nNo permanent State or Federal child safety-net program allocates dollars specifically for the purchase of diapers.\n#### 3. Inclusion of diapers as qualified medical expenses\n##### (a) Health savings accounts\nSection 223(d)(2) of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 is amended by adding at the end of subparagraph (A) the following: Notwithstanding the first sentence, amounts paid for diapers shall be treated as paid for medical care. .\n##### (b) Archer MSA s\nSection 220(d)(2)(A) of such Code is amended by adding at the end the following: Notwithstanding the first sentence, amounts paid for diapers shall be treated as paid for medical care. .\n##### (c) Health flexible spending arrangements and health reimbursement arrangements\nSection 106 of such Code is amended by adding at the end the following new subsection:\n(h) Reimbursements for diapers For purposes of this section and section 105, expenses incurred for diapers shall be treated as incurred for medical care. .\n##### (d) Dependent care assistance and dependent care flexible spending arrangements\nSection 129(e)(1) of such Code is amended by adding at the end the following: Such term shall include expenses incurred for diapers. .\n##### (e) Limited purpose flexible spending arrangements and health reimbursement arrangements\nSection 223(c)(1)(B) of such Code is amended by striking and at the end of clause (ii), by striking the period at the end of clause (iii) and inserting , and , and by adding at the end the following new clause:\n(iv) coverage under a flexible spending arrangement, or health reimbursement arrangement, that pays or reimburses for coverage described in clause (ii) (other than long-term care services and, in the case of a flexible spending arrangement, other than through insurance).
