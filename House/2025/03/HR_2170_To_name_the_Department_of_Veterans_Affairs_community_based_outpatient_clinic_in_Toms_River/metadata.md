It was heavily defended by the Germans and the mission of Colonel James Earl Rudder\u2019s 225 Rangers was to disable five 155-millimeter artillery guns, which could have rained artillery fire on the American forces landing on either beach, jeopardizing the invasion.\n**(7)**\nUnknown to the American forces, the guns had been moved from the tip of the Pointe and hidden, pointed in the direction of Utah Beach, in an inland apple orchard nearly a mile from the coastal batteries.\n**(8)**\nFirst Sergeant Lomell and another Ranger, Sergeant Jack Kuhn, found the guns undefended, and, with Kuhn providing cover, Lomell destroyed the weapons with thermite grenades and used his rifle butt to smash the sights of the guns.\n**(9)**\nFirst Sergeant Lomell subsequently received a battlefield promotion to Second Lieutenant, and was awarded some of the Army\u2019s highest honors, including the Distinguished Service Cross, Purple Heart, and Silver Star.\n**(10)**\nRenowned historian Steven Ambrose regarded First Sergeant Lomell\u2019s valiant acts along with General Eisenhower\u2019s decision to invade on June 6, 1944, as the most significant actions ensuring the Allied victory on D-Day.\n**(11)**\nFollowing World War II, Second Lieutenant Lomell was appointed as the First Director of the newly organized Ocean County Veteran\u2019s Service Bureau, serving from 1946 until 1948, before he attended law school on the G.I.
